# STAR TREK: THE LONG GAME
## Scene 3 — "THE DINNER"


INT. ENTERPRISE-A — CAPTAIN'S QUARTERS — NIGHT


Small. Lived-in. A table set for four with the particular care of someone who knows how to make a modest space feel generous.

KIRK sits at the head of the table. His uniform jacket is off — a command undershirt, and something in his posture that doesn't appear anywhere else on this ship. He's at rest here. As much as he's ever at rest.

McCOY pours wine. He's already told one story we didn't quite hear and is still smiling at the end of it.

SPOCK sits across from McCoy. A glass of water in front of him that he hasn't touched. He appears to be paying mild attention to the room.

He is paying absolute attention to everything.

A CHIME at the door.


                         KIRK
          Come.

The door opens. HUMMER enters.

He's in full dress uniform — the only one at the table who is. He reads the room in approximately half a second and knows he got it wrong.


                         McCOY
          Lose the jacket and sit down.

Hummer removes the jacket. Folds it over a chair back with the precision of someone for whom disorder has always had real consequences. Sits.


                         HUMMER
          I wasn't sure of the agenda.


                         McCOY
          The agenda is: table for four, one bottle, informal, no agenda.

Kirk leans forward and pours for Hummer. Catches his eyes for half a second.


                         KIRK
          We're off duty.

          Two words. The Shatner delivery — casual, warm, absolute. Not a suggestion. A command to stop being at attention.

Hummer settles. Looks at the table. The food. And for just a moment — half a second, maybe less — he stops.

Real meat. Not synthesized. Not obviously replicated. Something that had a life.

He says nothing. Picks up his fork.

Kirk noticed. He always notices.


                         McCOY (CONT'D)
               (reaching for the water dispenser)
          Now. The question of hydration.

He pours a glass. Holds it to the light above the table.

Grey. Slightly cloudy.

He regards it with the expression of a man who has been personally wronged by an inanimate object.


                         McCOY (CONT'D)
          I'm a doctor not a plumber.

He sets it down. Pours wine instead.

Nobody at the table comments on the grey water. They've all gotten used to it. Which is, somehow, the most eloquent political statement anyone has made all evening.


                         HUMMER
               (quietly — looking at the glass)
          That's from the filtration membrane?


                         McCOY
          Backorder. Three weeks and counting. Apparently someone decided the water supply of the Federation flagship is a discretionary expense.


                         KIRK
          And the crew hasn't complained once.


                         McCOY
          The crew is drinking fog.


                         KIRK
          Federation priorities.

Another beat. Something between Kirk and McCoy that isn't quite bitterness and isn't quite humor — the specific texture of men who have watched institutions they love make small foolish decisions for a long time and developed a private language for the feeling.

Hummer looks at his glass. Holds it up to the light — the faint milky opacity of water that has passed through a membrane that isn't doing its job anymore.


                         HUMMER
          Filtration membrane degradation?

McCoy turns. Slightly surprised.


                         McCOY
          How would you know that?


                         HUMMER
          Vega Colony water looked like this for about four years. You get used to it.

               (beat)

          I just didn't expect to see it on a Constitution-class ship.

A small silence. The specific silence of a man who has revealed something about where he came from without meaning to.


                         HUMMER (CONT'D)
          If you add a trace amount of calcium hypochlorite to the glass — just the glass, not the system — it binds to the particulate and precipitates out in about thirty seconds. You get one clear glass. The membrane residue settles to the —


                         McCOY
          — bottom as a calcium carbonate film.

He stops. Stares at Hummer. Then at his own glass. The look of a man who has been a doctor for thirty years and has just been shown a field medic's trick that works better than his complaint.


                         McCOY (CONT'D)
          That's — yes. That's basic chemistry. I should have —


                         HUMMER
          It doesn't scale. You can't dose the whole ship's supply — the concentration would have to be maintained dynamically and the carbonate buildup would clog the secondary —


                         McCOY
          No, I know. I know why it doesn't scale. I'm annoyed that I didn't think of it for one glass.

Kirk watches this exchange. Says nothing. Files something.


                         McCOY (CONT'D)
               (to Hummer, changing the subject with the ease of a man who has been changing subjects his whole career)
          Where are you from?


                         HUMMER
          Vega Colony. Originally.


                         McCOY
          Originally.


                         HUMMER
          I left when I was sixteen.

          McCoy's eyebrows register this without asking the question.


                         HUMMER (CONT'D)
          Freight hauler. I needed to save for the application fees.

A small silence. The particular silence that follows something true.


                         McCOY
          I'm from Georgia. My father was a doctor. His father was a doctor. I became a doctor because it simply never occurred to me not to.

               (beat)

          It took me about twenty years to understand how much of my life that sentence explains.


                         HUMMER
               (quietly)
          Nothing about Vega Colony made anything feel inevitable.


                         McCOY
          No. I imagine not.

Kirk has been listening. Not obviously — he's been eating, refilling glasses, present in the surface conversation. But his attention has the particular quality it gets when he's reading someone. Not assessing. Reading.

Hummer is beginning to relax. Slowly — the way people who have learned that relaxing can cost them eventually allow themselves to anyway.


                         KIRK
          You worked the asteroid belt out here? Before the Academy?


                         HUMMER
          Two years. Mining crew. It's —

               (a small pause)

          It's actually this system. Cordia. Same belt.

Something moves behind Kirk's eyes. Very briefly. He doesn't look at Spock.


                         KIRK
          Is that right.


                         HUMMER
          I didn't realize until I looked at the mission briefing. The drift patterns looked familiar.

               (almost to himself)

          I've cut rock out of those asteroids. The ones on the inner ring. You had to be careful with them — the composition varies, you think you're getting a clean calve and then —

He stops. Looks up. Slightly self-conscious — he's been talking about asteroid mining at a senior officers' dinner table.


                         McCOY
          No — go on. I find I prefer stories about actual work to most of what I hear at this table.


                         HUMMER
          It's not —


                         KIRK
          The inner ring composition varies how?

          And something in that question — the specific precision of it, the fact that Kirk actually wants the answer — relaxes something in Hummer that hasn't been relaxed in a long time.


                         HUMMER
          The outer rocks are mostly silicate. Predictable. You can calve them almost to spec. But the inner ring — older material, denser, the iron content creates these pockets where the stress fractures don't follow the crystal line. You'd fire at a face expecting a controlled shear and instead you'd get this —

He makes a gesture with his hands. An explosion. Debris in multiple vectors simultaneously.


                         HUMMER (CONT'D)
          You couldn't mine them the normal way. Couldn't just cut into them with phasers — the stress pockets would blow and you'd lose half the yield. Sometimes the whole rock.

               (beat)

          So you'd pack it first. Demolition charges — military-grade, shaped detonation. You'd drill six, maybe eight bore holes along the grain of the iron and set a ring pattern. Then you'd blow it.

He picks up a piece of bread from the table. Holds it flat. Snaps it in half — clean, along the center.


                         HUMMER (CONT'D)
          Splits right down the middle. Like breaking hardtack. Two clean halves, stress pockets vented, and the mining phasers can do the rest without any surprises.

He puts the bread down. Realizes he just used the captain's dinner as a geology demonstration. Slightly embarrassed.


                         McCOY
               (dry)
          I was going to eat that.


                         HUMMER (CONT'D)
          You learned to read the surface. The discoloration patterns that told you where the pockets were. Took about six months before you stopped getting surprised by them.


                         McCOY
          And after six months?


                         HUMMER
          After six months you knew exactly which rocks to aim for and which ones to leave alone.

Kirk is looking at him with an expression that has no name exactly. Interest, yes. Recognition, possibly. Something else.


                         KIRK
          The neighbouring colony. What was it like when you were working the belt?

The question lands somewhere unexpected. Hummer is quiet for a moment.


                         HUMMER
          Rough.

               (beat — deciding how much to say)

          No services. No real law enforcement. The medical facility was a prefab unit with one doctor who was there because she owed someone a favor. There were no families — nobody brought children to that place. Everybody was there because they couldn't afford to be somewhere else. You worked the belt, you drank what they sold you, you tried not to get into a fight, and you saved up enough to leave.

               (quieter)

          It was like — the old frontier stories. The ones where the town doesn't have a name yet because nobody expects it to last long enough to need one.


                         McCOY
          And the violence?


                         HUMMER
          Every payday. Sometimes between paydays. Half the crews were Klingon back then — they had their own approach to shift changes.

               (a careful pause — choosing his words)

          Good workers when they wanted to be. Equipment didn't always survive the enthusiasm.

               (beat)

          There was no security, so you handled it yourself or you didn't handle it. I was eighteen. I handled it.

He says it without bravado. The voice of a man stating the weather.


                         KIRK
          And now?


                         HUMMER
          I saw the current population figures in the mission briefing.


                         KIRK
          And?


                         HUMMER
          They've tripled. The infrastructure —

He stops.


                         KIRK
          Go ahead.


                         HUMMER
          The infrastructure in the briefing images is extraordinary. Schools. Medical center that looks like it belongs on a core world.

               (very quiet)

          I worked alongside people from that colony for two years. They didn't have any of that.

               (beat)

          The Cardassians built it.

Nobody says anything for a moment.


                         McCOY
          The colony's replicators are Cardassian-spec. Thirty years of infrastructure investment and the best thing they built is a food system. Three hundred thousand Federation citizens eating better than the crew of this ship.


                         KIRK
          Don't tell the crew.


                         McCOY
          They know. They always know.

Silence. The ship sounds around them.

Spock, who has eaten quietly through all of this, sets down his utensil with a precision that draws no particular attention.


                         SPOCK
          Mr. Hummer.

The room shifts. Not physically. Just the quality of attention in it.


                         HUMMER
          Commander.


                         SPOCK
          A hypothetical.


                         HUMMER
          Of course.


                         SPOCK
          You are in command of a vessel. The system is contested. Sensor range is severely degraded — let us say forty kilometers. The terrain is dense — asteroidal ring formation. Older material. Variable composition. Inner ring.

The smallest pause. Hummer doesn't register it consciously. But something in him is already leaning into the problem in a particular way.


                         SPOCK (CONT'D)
          Your vessel is positioned in what might generously be described as friendly adjacent space. A hostile vessel — superior firepower, exact position unknown — is somewhere in the ring. It has seen you. You have not seen it. It will move against you within minutes.

               (a beat exactly long enough)

          You have very limited options. What do you do?

Silence.

McCoy takes a sip of wine.

Kirk appears to be studying the middle distance.

Hummer is quiet. Not the quiet of someone searching for an answer. The quiet of someone deciding whether to say the answer they actually have.


                         HUMMER
          I would render every ship in the system unable to move.

A beat.


                         McCOY
          Including yours?


                         HUMMER
          Including mine.

Longer silence.

Kirk's face does something. An involuntary tightening around the eyes — the almost-invisible reaction of a man who is briefly, privately worried about someone. McCoy catches it. McCoy looks back at his plate.


                         SPOCK
          Elaborate.


                         HUMMER
          The hostile vessel has position and firepower. If I fight on its terms I lose. If I run I lose. Both assume the battle is about firepower and position.

               (beat)

          It doesn't have to be about that.

          If I can make every ship in the system immobile — including mine — then the battle becomes about something else. Where are we? Whose support arrives first?

          If I'm in friendly adjacent space — my reinforcements come, theirs don't. I don't have to win the fight. I have to make the fight pointless.

               (beat)

          The hostile commander has to break the immobility himself — which costs him — or wait for help that isn't coming. Either way it stops being his game.

He stops. Slightly self-conscious again — as if hearing himself at full volume for the first time.


                         HUMMER (CONT'D)
          In a ring formation — if I knew the orbital mechanics well enough — the asteroids themselves could create the immobility. You wouldn't even need to fire at the enemy. Fire at the geometry. Close the corridors. Let physics do it.

A long silence.

Spock's expression hasn't changed. It never does. But something about the angle of his attention has shifted — the way a musician's body changes when they hear something unexpected from another instrument.

McCoy is looking at his plate.

Kirk is very still.


                         KIRK
               (almost to himself)
          That would absolutely never work.

          Three seconds. Four.


                         KIRK (CONT'D)
          Except that it would.

He reaches for the wine. Tops up Hummer's glass without asking. Changes the subject completely.


                         KIRK (CONT'D)
          McCoy, you were telling a story earlier with no ending.


                         McCOY
          It has an ending. The ending requires seventeen minutes of context.


                         KIRK
          Summarize.


                         McCOY
          A man, a compromised situation, a decision that was technically correct and entirely wrong.


                         KIRK
          That's most of Starfleet.


                         McCOY
          That's most of everything.

The dinner continues. Hummer fully relaxed now — McCoy being funny, Kirk asking questions that seem idle, Spock occasionally deploying an observation with the dry precision of someone who has calibrated exactly how much wit is socially appropriate.

Hummer is speaking without auditing himself.

He doesn't know that is the point.


LATER.


The plates are cleared. The second bottle is mostly gone. Hummer stands, thanks them — genuine, slightly formal, the residue of formality that survives even a genuinely good evening.

He retrieves his folded jacket. Pauses at the door.


                         HUMMER
               (to McCoy)

          The filtration membranes. I used to patch similar units on the haulers — there's a trick with the ion exchange layer that sometimes buys you a few more weeks. Doesn't fix them. But it might take the fog down a grade or two.


                         McCOY
          It's worth a try.


                         HUMMER
          I'll take a look at them tomorrow.

He leaves.

The door closes.

McCoy sits back in his chair with the particular posture of a man who has just had a good evening and knows it.


                         McCOY
          I like him.

Kirk says nothing. Turns his empty glass slowly on the table. Looking at it.


                         McCOY (CONT'D)
          Don't let him get himself killed.

Kirk looks at his glass. Picks it up. Turns it slowly in his hand — the grey water catching what light there is. The particular gesture of a man weighing something that doesn't fit in a glass.


                         KIRK
          He'll be alright.

Kirk looks up. Toward the door Hummer left through. He doesn't say anything.

Spock stands. Moves toward the door with the economy of ceremony he brings to every departure.

Kirk looks at Spock.

Just for a moment.

Spock meets the look.

Nothing is said.

But Kirk understood in that moment exactly what Spock did tonight — the question built around density gradients and calving patterns of a specific belt in a specific system, asked of a specific man who spent two years learning that belt with his hands.

The seed planted in the only soil where it would grow.

Memory.

Spock leaves.

Kirk sits alone at the table with an empty bottle and a glass of grey water neither of them drank.
