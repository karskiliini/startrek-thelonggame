# STAR TREK: THE LONG GAME
## *Grey Water*

# OPENING CREDITS — "THE ROCKS"

---

`EXT. ASTEROID BELT — CORDIA SYSTEM — SPACE`

Silence.

Stars. The particular darkness between them.

Then — rock.

A single asteroid filling the frame. No sense of scale yet. Just texture. Craters and ridges and the slow patient turning of something very old in the light of a distant sun.

The camera pulls back.

More asteroids. Dozens. Hundreds. Each one tumbling in its slow orbital dance. The belt spreads across the frame and keeps spreading — a river of ancient rock circling a moon that is itself circling a planet we can't see yet.

Still no sense of scale.

Then — movement.

Small at first. Barely visible. A shuttle, working its way between two rocks. Then another. Then three more, spread across the belt at different distances.

Working shuttles. Not exploration vessels. Not Starfleet grey. These are mining craft — battered, patched, the specific ugliness of tools that have been used hard and maintained just enough. Each one moves with careful deliberation between the rocks.

Their phasers fire.

Not the dramatic beam of a weapon. A working tool — focused, narrow, cutting against a rock face with the patience of someone who has done this ten thousand times and will do it ten thousand more.

Now we understand the scale.

The shuttle is the size of a thumbnail against the rock it's cutting. The rock it's cutting is the size of a city. The cut it's making in the city-sized rock is a thin precise line that will take months to deepen into something useful.

This work is measured in years.

A TITLE CARD appears. Simple. White on black.

> **STAR TREK: THE LONG GAME**
> *Grey Water*

The credits begin. Unhurried. The work continues underneath them.

The camera moves slowly through the belt — drifting past rocks the size of continents, past shuttles small as dust motes against them, past the patient geometry of people who come back shift after shift after shift to cut at something that will outlast them by a billion years regardless.

There is no music yet. Just the silence of space and, very faintly, the sound design of the shuttles — vibration through hull, the mechanical rhythm of the phaser cutting system cycling, the nothing-sound of vacuum.

The last credit fades.

The camera begins to rise.

Up through the belt. Up past the outermost rocks. Up until the moon comes into frame below — pale, rocky, unremarkable except for one thing: a canyon system on its surface, visible even from this distance. A dark wound in the pale rock. A deep system of channels cut by some ancient geological event, running for hundreds of kilometers across the moon's face.

The camera keeps rising.

Beyond the moon. And there —

The planet.

Green and blue and alive. Cloud systems moving across its surface in slow deliberate patterns. And even from orbit — even at this distance — something organized is visible. The geometry of infrastructure. Roads, power distribution, agricultural patterning. A civilization that has been invested in. A world that looks cared for.

It looks like somewhere people chose to build something.

The camera holds on the planet for one long breath.

On the dark side of the terminator — the night edge where the sun hasn't reached yet — the geometry of city lights is visible. Grids and clusters. The particular pattern of a civilization that keeps the lights on.

Then — a flash. A single point of light, brief and sharp, in the middle of one of the larger industrial clusters.

It disappears.

And a section of the grid — perhaps a quarter of that cluster — goes dark.

The camera doesn't move. Doesn't zoom. Doesn't acknowledge it.

We don't know yet what we're looking at.
