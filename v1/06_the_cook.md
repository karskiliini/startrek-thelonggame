# STAR TREK: THE LONG GAME
## Scene 6 — "THE COOK"


INT. CARDASSIAN VESSEL — MAREK'S QUARTERS — CONTINUOUS


Marek is in his quarters. He has been cooking — the apron is on, the knives in their sheaths, a half-prepared meal on the counter. He was making something when the explosion happened.

He is standing very still. The kind of still that comes from a man keeping everything he feels below the surface through sheer discipline.

The display in front of him shows the aftermath. The power distribution hub in the northern settlement. His hub. Three years of work. Rubble.

He opens a channel. Encrypted. Private.


SPLIT SCREEN — MAREK / K'TAGH


K'Tagh appears. Behind him, his bridge. He is standing — not with the posture of a man who has done something. With the posture of a man who has been woken by something he doesn't understand and is already angry about it.


                         MAREK
          Commander. I'm looking at the northern power hub.

               (beat)

          Or what remains of it.


                         K'TAGH
          I see it.


                         MAREK
          The energy signature in the debris is Klingon.

A silence. K'Tagh's face changes — not guilt. Something rawer. Confusion becoming fury.


                         K'TAGH
          I did not fire on your settlement.


                         MAREK
          The frequency is Klingon. The weapon is Klingon. Six of my people are dead.


                         K'TAGH
          Your Federation overlords arrived this morning. By tonight your hub is rubble. And you call me?

He is so arrogant that Marek cannot tell if he is deflecting or if he genuinely doesn't know. This is the problem with Klingons — their authentic emotions and their performed emotions are identical.


                         MAREK
               (a pause — then, with the particular patience of a man explaining something to a child)
          Commander. The Federation did not attack a Cardassian installation on Federation colonial territory. They don't have the stomach for it. They don't have the institutional will.


                         K'TAGH
          Then explain the timing.


                         MAREK
          The timing is what concerns me. The Enterprise arrives — and within hours, someone with a Klingon weapon attacks Cardassian infrastructure. If I were a suspicious man, I would say that someone wants the Federation to see Klingon aggression.

               (beat)

          That someone is either very stupid or very strategic. Which are you, Commander?


K'Tagh's jaw works. His hands are fists at his sides. And then — because he is K'Tagh, because he has spent twenty-two days in orbit doing nothing while his crew rusts and his purpose rots — he says the thing he should not say.


                         K'TAGH
          You think this corridor belongs to you. You think your schools and your hospitals and your thirty years of investment make this place Cardassian.

               (leaning into the screen)

          The High Council does not agree. The High Council has a different timeline for this system. And when that timeline arrives — your schools and your hospitals will be footnotes.


A beat. Marek's face does not change. But something behind his eyes does.


                         MAREK
          A different timeline.


                         K'TAGH
          You heard me.


                         MAREK
          The High Council has a timeline for a system that Cardassia has built from nothing. A system where sixty percent of the population is Cardassian. Where every road, every hospital, every power grid was designed, funded, and maintained by Cardassian engineers.

               (beat — very quiet)

          And the Empire intends to take it.


                         K'TAGH
          The Empire intends to claim what the Empire has always claimed. This corridor. This system. The resources in that belt. You were useful. You built the infrastructure. The infrastructure will remain.

               (the grin — the terrible grin)

          You will not.

The silence that follows is the longest silence in the film.

Marek is looking at K'Tagh. He is looking at the face of a man who has just told him — without meaning to, without caring — that thirty years of Cardassian investment was preparation for someone else's harvest.

He built the house. The Klingons intend to move in.


                         MAREK
          Thank you, Commander. You have been remarkably candid.

He closes the channel.

The screen goes dark.


Marek stands in front of the dark screen for three seconds. Then he moves. The composure is still there — harder now, operational. He opens a different channel. A channel that goes much further than the Klingon warbird in orbit.

A channel that reaches Cardassia Prime.


INT. CARDASSIAN VESSEL — MAREK'S QUARTERS — MOMENTS LATER


The seal of the Cardassian Central Command fills the screen. Then — a face. Not a military officer. A civilian. A LEGATE in a well-lit office on a planet that is very far away from here.

He looks at Marek the way bureaucrats look at field commanders — with the particular discomfort of a man who has been interrupted by something that is not on his schedule.


                         MAREK
          Legate. The situation at Cordia has changed. The Klingon Empire intends to seize the system. I have this from their own commander.

               (beat)

          I need a military response. A warship. Troops. Something that tells the High Council that Cardassia does not abandon its investments.


The Legate listens. He listens the way men in comfortable offices listen to men in dangerous places — patiently, carefully, with the full intention of doing nothing.


                         LEGATE
          Gul Marek. The political situation on Cardassia Prime is — delicate. The détente with the Klingon Empire is a priority of the Central Command. A military deployment to a disputed system at this time would be seen as a provocation.


                         MAREK
          Legate — they are planning to take a system with three hundred thousand Cardassian nationals. A system we built.


                         LEGATE
          You have done extraordinary work at Cordia. The Central Command is aware of your contributions. But the strategic calculus at this level requires —


                         MAREK
          Requires what?


                         LEGATE
               (the voice of a man who has made his decision before the conversation started)
          Requires patience. Continue the diplomatic track. Work with the Federation. File your reports. The Central Command will review the situation at the appropriate time.

               (beat)

          That is our position, Gul.

Our position. The words of a government that has already decided that Cordia is not worth a war. That thirty years of infrastructure and three hundred thousand people are an acceptable loss if the alternative is a confrontation with the Klingon Empire.

Marek knows this voice. He has used this voice himself — the voice of a man explaining to someone that their life's work is being weighed against something larger and found lighter.


                         MAREK
          Legate. With respect. If the Central Command does not act, I will lose this colony. Everything we built. The schools. The hospitals. The corridor.


                         LEGATE
          You will not lose the colony, Gul. You will transition to a revised operational framework. The details will be communicated through standard channels.

Revised operational framework. The Cardassian way of saying retreat.


                         MAREK
          I understand.

Two words. He closes the channel.

The screen goes dark. Again.


Marek stands in his quarters. The half-prepared meal on the counter. The smell of something that was going to be dinner. The apron — his grandfather's leather, the knives in their sheaths, the weight of it across his chest like something that has always been there.

The Klingons are coming for his colony. And Cardassia Prime has told him, in the most polished language available, to accept it.

Thirty years.

He reaches up. Unties the apron. Slowly. The way a man unties something he has worn every day of his adult life. The leather is warm from his body. The knives shift in their sheaths as it comes free.

He holds it for a moment. Looks at it. The worn fold lines from generations of the same gesture. His grandfather's hands. His father's hands. His hands.

Then he throws it. Not hard. Not with rage. With exhaustion. The exhaustion of a man who has just learned that everything he built was for nothing. The apron hits the far wall — the knives barely rattling, the heavy leather sliding down to the floor — and lies there in a heap.

Marek looks at it.

He doesn't pick it up.

He stands there for a long time. A man in a room that smells like a meal no one will eat, looking at an apron on the floor that no one will wear.

Then — very slowly — he turns to the communications console. Opens a third channel. Not Cardassia Prime. Not the Klingons.

Something else.

The cook has left the kitchen.
