# STAR TREK: THE LONG GAME
## Scene 8 — "SORAK"


INT. STARBASE 47 — OFFICERS' LOUNGE


A small starbase near the Vega system. Not Starbase 12 — something smaller, more remote. The kind of station that exists because logistics requires a place to refuel between here and there.

Enterprise is docked for refuelling. The crew stretches its legs in corridors that smell marginally better than their own.

Kirk enters the officers' lounge. It's quiet. A few officers from the station. A bartender who looks like she's been here longer than the station.

SORAK is sitting at a corner table. A drink in front of him — untouched. He is somewhere between fifty and ageless. He stands the way intelligence officers stand — present without being noticeable.

Kirk sees him. Stops.

Something passes across Kirk's face — surprise, warmth, and something else. The particular alertness of a man who has learned that coincidences involving Sorak are never coincidences.


                         KIRK
          Sorak.


                         SORAK
          Jim.

               (the smile reaches his eyes — or appears to)

          Of all the starbases in all the sectors.

Kirk sits. The ease of two men who have shared enough history that the small talk is optional.


                         KIRK
          You just happened to be at this starbase.


                         SORAK
          I travel. You know that.


                         KIRK
          You travel where you need to be.


                         SORAK
          That is the nature of travel.

A beat. Kirk doesn't push. He knows Sorak well enough to know that pushing produces exactly nothing.

The bartender brings Kirk a drink without being asked. Kirk looks at it. Looks at Sorak.


                         SORAK (CONT'D)
          I ordered for you. I hope you don't mind. The bourbon here is surprisingly adequate.

Kirk takes a sip. Studies the man across from him. The friend he trusts. The intelligence officer he watches.


                         KIRK
          I've just come from Vega.


                         SORAK
          I know.


                         KIRK
          And before that, Arctura III.


                         SORAK
          I know that too.

               (a beat — the warmth settling into something more careful)

          What did you see?


                         KIRK
          You know what I saw. You've been watching this sector longer than I've been assigned to it.


                         SORAK
          I've been watching this sector for fifteen years.

He says it without emphasis. A fact. Fifteen years of data, contacts, favours, and calculations — condensed into one sentence over bourbon.


                         KIRK
          A lot of good officers haven't made it that far.

It lands gently. But it lands.


                         SORAK
          No. They haven't.

               (beat)

          But every one of them died for something that mattered. I have to believe that. Otherwise none of this means anything.

He says it looking directly at Kirk. The warmth in his eyes is real. The calculus behind it is also real. He is speaking to a man he respects, admires, and has already factored into an equation that has an acceptable cost.


                         KIRK
          What aren't you telling me?


                         SORAK
          Many things. As always.

               (beat — then, quieter)

          Jim. Cordia is not Arctura. It's not Vega. What you saw on those worlds — the demographic shift, the infrastructure capture, the slow erasure — on Cordia that process is complete. The Federation flag is still flying but there is no Federation authority on the ground.

               (beat)

          Three hundred thousand Cardassian nationals. Klingon military operations in the belt. The human population confined to sectors with diminishing services. And a Klingon warbird in orbit that has been there for months.


                         KIRK
          The Rek'thar. Commander K'Tagh.


                         SORAK
          You've read the file.


                         KIRK
          Spock briefed us. His record is "distinguished."


                         SORAK
          That is one word for it. The Klingons have another.

               (beat)

          Be careful with the rogues, Jim.


                         KIRK
          Rogues.


                         SORAK
          The belt. The cargo traffic through there has tripled. Nobody moves that much material through asteroids for humanitarian purposes.

He finishes his drink. Sets the glass down with the particular care of a man who has said exactly as much as he intended to say.


                         SORAK (CONT'D)
          I wish we were seeing each other under better circumstances. But with us, it always seems to be like this.


                         KIRK
               (the half-smile of a man who has heard this before)
          I thought you would've retired by now. I hear Vulcan is nice this time of year.


                         SORAK
          I've been told.

A silence. Two men at a table. Bourbon. The particular warmth of a friendship that has survived things that friendships don't usually survive.


                         KIRK
          It's good to see you.


                         SORAK
          And you, Jim.

Kirk stands. They don't shake hands. They don't need to.

He walks toward the door. Stops.


                         KIRK
          Sorak. Whatever you're not telling me — I'll find out eventually. I always do.


                         SORAK
          I'm counting on it.

Kirk leaves.

Sorak sits alone at the table. The smile is gone. He looks at Kirk's empty glass. The glass of a man he just sent toward something he cannot warn him about — because the warning would change the outcome, and the outcome is what matters.

He has done this before. With other men. In other lounges. At other starbases.

Not all of them came back.
