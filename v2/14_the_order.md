# STAR TREK: THE LONG GAME
## Scene 14 — "THE ORDER"


INT. KLINGON WARBIRD — K'TAGH'S QUARTERS — ENCRYPTED CHANNEL


K'Tagh's quarters are not like Marek's. No cooking surface. No family heirlooms. A cot. A weapons rack. A table with a bottle on it that is already half empty.

The display shows a face — GENERAL KORD, Klingon High Command. Old. Scarred. The kind of face that has survived everything and remembers none of it fondly.

The channel is encrypted with Klingon military protocols. No one else can hear this conversation. For the first time in the film, K'Tagh is not performing.


                         K'TAGH
          The Cardassian has closed the shipping lanes. A safety inspection. The kind that takes months.


                         GENERAL KORD
          What does that mean for our installations?


                         K'TAGH
          If the supply line stays closed — six weeks before the energy stores run dry. Eight weeks before the weapons systems lose maintenance capability. After that, the bases are dead. Everything we built in those rocks becomes — scrap.

               (beat)

          And it stays there. In asteroids that sit inside a system controlled by Cardassians. They will find it eventually. They will take it apart. They will use it or destroy it. Either way, it becomes theirs.


A silence. The General's face does nothing.


                         GENERAL KORD
          How much have we invested?


                         K'TAGH
          Seven years. Forty-three shipments diverted from the mining quota. Enough weapons-grade material to arm a forward battle group. Sensor installations covering the entire corridor approach.

               (quieter)

          It was supposed to be our guarantee. If the alliance failed — if the Cardassians turned on us — we would have had a position they couldn't take without a war they couldn't afford.


                         GENERAL KORD
          And now a trade embargo threatens to hand it to them for free.


                         K'TAGH
          Marek doesn't know about the installations. Not yet. But his embargo will force my ships away from the belt. Once my patrols are gone, his engineers will survey the asteroids. They survey everything.


The General shifts. The scar on his jaw catches the light.


                         GENERAL KORD
          We are sending the IKS Vor'nak. Commander Toral. He will arrive in thirty-six hours.


                         K'TAGH
          One ship —


                         GENERAL KORD
          One ship is enough. The Vor'nak carries enough firepower to destroy the Cardassian diplomatic vessel and neutralize their orbital command structure. The colony administration collapses without Marek. His embargo dies with him.

               (beat — the particular tone of a general who has given this kind of order before)

          Remove the Cardassian. Re-establish Klingon authority in the system. The High Council has determined that Klingon interests in the Cordia sector have been subordinated to Cardassian convenience for long enough.


K'Tagh is quiet for a moment. Re-establish Klingon authority. As though they ever had it. As though the system was ever theirs — instead of a corner they were allowed to occupy while someone else built the real infrastructure.

But the word works because it does what it needs to do: it gives K'Tagh the authority to act, wrapped in the fiction that he is restoring something rather than seizing it.


                         K'TAGH
          The Enterprise is in the system. Kirk.


                         GENERAL KORD
          Kirk is irrelevant. He is here to observe. If he chooses to protect the Cardassian vessel — that is his decision and his consequence.


                         K'TAGH
          He will protect it.


                         GENERAL KORD
          Then he will learn what Klingons do when their interests are threatened.

               (beat)

          This is not a negotiation, K'Tagh. This is an order. The Cardassian position in the Cordia system ends. If the Federation involves itself — that is a separate problem. One we have contingencies for.

The General's face is the face of a government that has already decided and is informing the instrument of its decision. The mad dog doctrine in its purest form — except this time the order is explicit, not implied.


                         K'TAGH
          Understood.

He closes the channel.


K'Tagh sits alone in his quarters. The bottle. The weapons rack. The encrypted silence of a room where an order was just given that cannot be taken back.

He picks up the bottle. Pours. Not celebration. The particular drinking of a man who has just received permission to do the thing he was going to do anyway — and finds that the permission doesn't taste the way he expected.

He wanted to act on his own authority. Now he is acting on theirs. The difference matters to a Klingon. The difference between a warrior and a tool.

Marek's word. Tool.

He drinks.
