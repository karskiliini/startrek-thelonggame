# STAR TREK: THE LONG GAME
## Scene 15 — "KOBAYASHI MARU"


EXT. SPACE — CORDIA ASTEROID BELT — CONTINUOUS


Music. Not Federation. Not the orchestral warmth of a Starfleet fanfare.

Klingon.

Percussion first — deep, hollow, the war drums of a species that builds instruments the way it builds weapons. Then brass. Low brass. The kind of sound that vibrates in the sternum before the ear registers it. A march. A hunting march. The music of something that has arrived and does not intend to leave.

The IKS Vor'nak fills the frame.

Close. Very close. The camera begins at the bow — the forward disruptor bank, a weapon system larger than most Federation shuttlecraft. The emitters glow faintly green, cycling at standby. Not firing. Not needing to. The glow is a promise, not a threat. The threat is the hull itself.

This is not K'Tagh's warbird.

K'Tagh's ship was a patrol vessel. Fast, aggressive, built for border skirmishes and intimidation. The Vor'nak is a cruiser. The difference is the difference between a knife and a hammer — K'Tagh's ship was designed to cut; this one is designed to break.

The hull is thicker. The nacelle pylons wider. The disruptor banks are doubled — port and starboard, dorsal and ventral, a weapons geometry that has no blind spot and no mercy in it. The deflector array at the bow is armored — a warship that expects to take hits and has been built to keep moving through them.

The camera tracks along the hull. Slow. The scale of it asserting itself meter by meter. Klingon hull plating — dark, angular, the particular aesthetic of a civilization that considers beauty and lethality to be the same word.

Shield generators cycling green across the surface. Full power. Everything at full power. A ship that has not fired a shot and has all the patience in the world.

The camera reaches the forward disruptor bank. Pauses there. The emitter array fills the frame — the barrel of a weapon that could gut a Constitution-class ship in three sustained volleys.

Then the camera shifts.

Not cutting. Drifting. The perspective slides sideways from the Vor'nak's bow, past the disruptor bank, and the background opens up behind the weapon — the vast dark geometry of the asteroid belt. Continent-sized rocks tumbling in their ancient orbits. The dust hazes. The deep corridors between the stones.

And there — between the rocks, far away, impossibly small against the scale of this place —

Enterprise.

The size of a postage stamp. A pale shape drifting among boulders that dwarf it a thousand times over. Listing slightly to starboard. One impulse engine glowing unevenly. The navigation array dark on one side. A ship that looks — at this distance, from this angle, framed by the Vor'nak's weapons — like something that wandered into the wrong part of the ocean.

A fly in a web. An insect caught between stones.

The Klingon music swells. Not louder. Deeper. The drums settling into a patient rhythm. The rhythm of something that has all the time it needs.


INT. IKS VOR'NAK — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


COMMANDER TORAL stands at the tactical display.

He is younger than K'Tagh. Calmer. The particular calm of a man who has never had to compensate for doubt with volume. He does not pace. He does not shout. He reads the display the way a surgeon reads a scan — looking for the detail that matters, discarding the rest.

The display shows Enterprise. The damage profile. The shield output. The engine signature.

He has everything he needs.


                         TORAL
               (to his tactical officer, without looking up)
          Time to weapons range?


                         VOR'NAK TACTICAL OFFICER
          Four minutes at current approach.


                         TORAL
          Reduce to half impulse. There is no hurry.

He looks at the display. The postage-stamp Enterprise drifting among the rocks. A ship that has clearly been in a fight and clearly lost it.

He received K'Tagh's automated distress beacon eleven minutes ago. He can see K'Tagh's warbird on the long-range display — tumbling, holed, dark. He does not know what happened to it. He does not particularly care. K'Tagh was sent here to hold a position and failed. Toral was sent here to finish what K'Tagh could not.

He intends to finish it quickly.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
          All weapons to full charge. Shields to maximum.

               (beat)

          Do not hail them.


INT. ENTERPRISE-A — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


Fire.

Not weapons fire. Actual fire. A conduit behind the science station has ruptured and something is burning — the acrid chemical smell of insulation that was never meant to be exposed to atmosphere. Two crewmen are on it with extinguishers, the white spray cutting through the smoke in sharp arcs. The overhead lighting flickers. Steadies. Flickers again.

KIRK in the command chair. Sweat on his forehead. Not the clean sweat of exertion — the damp, feverish sheen of a man whose body has started producing adrenaline it has nowhere to spend.

The viewer shows the Vor'nak. Distant. Moving — but not toward them. Moving laterally. Drifting across the field of view from left to right, unhurried, the particular trajectory of a predator that is not chasing its prey but positioning itself above it.

Not approaching. Circling.


                         PARK
          She's not closing.


                         SPOCK
          She is maneuvering to the dorsal aft quadrant. Our weakest shield coverage. The port generator cannot maintain full output in that arc.

Kirk watches the Vor'nak slide across the viewer. The ship is in no hurry. It is moving the way a chess piece moves — not toward the king, but to the square that makes the king impossible.

The optimal attack angle. From behind. From above. Where Enterprise cannot bring her forward phaser banks to bear and the damaged port shield generator leaves a gap the size of a shuttle bay.

Toral knows exactly what he's doing.


                         KIRK
          Transporters.


                         PARK
          Sir?


                         KIRK
          What's the status of the transporters?


                         PARK
               (checking)
          Transporter room two just came back online. Room one is still —


                         KIRK
          Get me Hummer. Now. Beam him off that shuttle.


                         PARK
          Sir — the shuttle is in the belt. The mineral interference at that range —


                         KIRK
          He's on a colonial transponder frequency. Lock on to that signal and get him aboard.

               (beat)

          Now, Mr. Park.

Park works the console. The fire behind the science station is out — the crewmen pulling the extinguishers away, the smoke being drawn into the ventilation system that is trying its best to function normally. The smell stays.


                         PARK
          Lock acquired. Signal is weak — the iron content in the surrounding —


                         KIRK
          Energize.


                         PARK
          Energizing.

A beat. Two beats. The transporter whine from somewhere below decks — the particular sound of matter being disassembled and reassembled, audible through the deck plates as a faint harmonic.


                         PARK (CONT'D)
          Transport complete. One passenger. Transporter room two.


                         KIRK
               (to communications)
          Transporter room — send Mr. Hummer to the bridge. Immediately.

The viewer. The Vor'nak has completed its lateral pass. It is now behind them and above — a position Enterprise cannot reach without turning the ship, and turning the ship means presenting the damaged port side to a cruiser with four disruptor banks.

Kirk looks at it. The look of a man watching the trap close and counting the seconds until the teeth meet.

The turbolift opens.

HUMMER steps out. Still in the mining shuttle flight suit — the grey coveralls of a colonial utility operator, not a Starfleet uniform. Dust on his hands. Rock dust in his hair. He has been in the belt for hours, reading stones, hiding in shadows.

He takes in the bridge in one glance. The smoke residue. The flickering consoles. The damage reports scrolling on every secondary display. Kirk's face.

The viewer.

He sees the Vor'nak. Behind and above. The green glow of weapons at full charge. The patient geometry of a ship that has already won and is simply choosing the moment.

He goes to the tactical station. Sits. His hands find the console the way a musician's hands find the instrument — not looking, not thinking, just arriving.

He pulls up the display.


                         HUMMER
               (quiet, reading the tactical situation)
          How long has she been here?


                         KIRK
          Seven minutes. She's not in a hurry.


                         HUMMER
          No. She wouldn't be.

He looks at the readings. Shield status. Weapons status. Engine status. The arithmetic of a fight they cannot win.

Then he looks at the asteroid belt on the long-range display. The rocks. The corridors. The inner ring where the iron-rich asteroids are — the ones with the discoloration patterns. The ones with the hidden Klingon installations.

He looks at it for a long time.


INT. CARDASSIAN VESSEL — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


MAREK stands at the communications console. His bridge is small — a diplomatic vessel, not a warship. Four crew. No weapons worth mentioning. The shields would hold against debris. They would not hold against anything else.

He has been watching. He saw K'Tagh's warbird holed by the rocks. He saw Enterprise limp past it. He saw the Vor'nak arrive.

He is performing the calculation he has performed every day for thirty years: what is the move that keeps the game going?

If Enterprise is destroyed here, the game ends. Not in his favor. Not in anyone's favor. A Federation flagship destroyed in a system where Cardassian infrastructure covers every surface — the Federation will send a battle group. The Klingons will escalate. Cardassia will be forced to choose a side. And everything he built — the schools, the hospitals, the colony that works — becomes collateral in someone else's war.

He opens a channel.


                         MAREK
          Commander Toral. This is Gul Marek, Cardassian diplomatic envoy to the Cordia system. I am requesting an immediate cessation of hostilities in this corridor pending —


INT. IKS VOR'NAK — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


Toral looks at the communications display. The Cardassian signal. The small diplomatic vessel on his tactical readout — it has moved, slightly, toward the space between his ship and Enterprise.

He does not look at his tactical officer. He does not look at anyone. He continues reading the Enterprise damage profile on his display.


                         MAREK (V.O.)
          — the legal and diplomatic framework governing military action in joint administrative zones requires consultation with —


                         TORAL
          Disruptors. The Cardassian.

Two words. Said the way a man says "pass the salt."


                         VOR'NAK TACTICAL OFFICER
          Sir — that is a diplomatic vessel. Cardassian registry. If we —


                         TORAL
          The Cardassian.

The tactical officer hesitates for one half-second. Then he fires.


EXT. SPACE — CONTINUOUS


Two disruptor beams. Green. Bright. The particular focused intensity of a weapons system designed to cut through military-grade shielding.

The Cardassian diplomatic vessel has no military-grade shielding.

The first beam passes through the shields as though they are not there — which, against this weapon, they functionally are not — and strikes the port hull. The ship doesn't explode. It buckles. The hull plating folds inward like paper, the structural frame visible for one instant before the energy discharge ignites the atmosphere inside.

The second beam hits the engineering section. The warp core containment fails.

The Cardassian vessel does not explode dramatically. It comes apart. Quickly, silently, the pieces separating in the vacuum with the specific tidiness of something that was never built to survive this. Hull fragments. Deck plating. The contents of rooms — furniture, equipment, personal effects — tumbling outward in an expanding sphere of debris that catches the distant sunlight and glitters.

It takes four seconds.

Marek was mid-sentence.


INT. KLINGON WARBIRD — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


K'Tagh's bridge. The emergency lighting. The slow rotation visible through the cracked viewport — stars wheeling, the belt turning, the particular vertigo of a ship that cannot stop spinning.

K'Tagh saw it. The Cardassian vessel — there, then not there. Two beams. Four seconds. He watched it happen from a viewport that faces the wrong direction half the time.

He knows that efficiency. He recognizes the doctrine. No warning. No communication. No waste.

Toral.

K'Tagh straightens. Wipes the blood from his brow with the back of his hand. He opens a channel to the Vor'nak. Encrypted. Commander to commander.


                         K'TAGH
          Commander Toral. This is K'Tagh. I engaged the Enterprise directly and struck their power generation systems. Their port shields are compromised. Their impulse drive is damaged. The ship you are pursuing is the ship I weakened.

He waits for acknowledgment. Nothing comes. The channel is open. Toral is listening. K'Tagh continues.


                         K'TAGH (CONT'D)
          I am repairing my inertial stabilizers. My engineer estimates three hours — I will have them in two. When I am operational, I will close from below while you maintain the dorsal position. The Enterprise cannot cover both arcs. If you approach from the upper aft quadrant, I will strike the ventral —

The channel closes.

Not with a word. Not with a signal. The connection simply terminates. The display goes dark. The sound of static replaces the sound of an open line.

K'Tagh stares at the dark screen.

Toral did not respond. Did not acknowledge the tactical proposal. Did not say no. Did not say anything. He listened to a wounded commander offer his service and closed the channel the way a man closes a door on a beggar.

K'Tagh's hand tightens on the console. The knuckles white. His face — for the first time in the film — is not angry. Not loud. Not performing.

He is humiliated.

The slow rotation of his ship continues. Stars wheel past the viewport. Somewhere out there, a battle is about to begin and K'Tagh will watch it happen through a cracked window on a ship that spins.


INT. ENTERPRISE-A — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


The bridge crew watches the Cardassian debris on the viewer. The glittering field expanding where a vessel with four crew and a diplomatic mission used to be.

Nobody speaks.

Kirk stares at the viewer. The debris. The expanding cloud of things that were a ship and are now nothing.

Marek. The apron. The knives. The sixty-eight vintage Kanar. The man who cooked for his enemies and built schools for their children and played a thirty-year game with more patience and more genuine care than anyone on either side of the table.

Gone. Mid-sentence. Four seconds.


                         McCOY
               (very quiet, from the back of the bridge)
          Jim.

Kirk doesn't respond. His eyes are on the debris field.

On the viewer, beyond the glittering remains, the Vor'nak is already turning. The Cardassian vessel was a three-second interruption. A footnote. Toral has returned his full attention to Enterprise.

Hummer looks at the tactical display. The Vor'nak is completing its arc. The optimal attack angle — dorsal aft — will be achieved in approximately ninety seconds.

He looks at the asteroid belt. The inner ring. The iron-rich rocks with the Klingon installations.

Ninety seconds.


                         HUMMER
               (quiet — to Kirk)
          Captain. I need you to trust me.

Kirk looks at him. The face of a man who just watched a friend die mid-sentence. The face of a man who is about to watch four hundred more die unless something changes in the next ninety seconds.


                         KIRK
          What do you need?


                         HUMMER
          Turn us around. Back into the belt. Full impulse — whatever the port engine can give us.


                         KIRK
          Into the belt? We just —


                         HUMMER
          I know what's in those rocks. Toral doesn't.

Kirk looks at him. Looks at the viewer. The Vor'nak closing its arc. Eighty seconds.

He doesn't ask for an explanation. There isn't time.


                         KIRK
          Helm — one-eighty. Back into the belt. Everything we've got.

Enterprise turns. The great ship groaning as the port engine strains to match the starboard — the hull shuddering with the asymmetric thrust, the inertial dampeners struggling to compensate.

She turns.

And runs.

On the viewer — the asteroid belt. The rocks. The ancient stones that Hummer spent two years learning to read.

Behind them — the Vor'nak. Following. No longer circling. Pursuing.

Toral watches Enterprise run toward the rocks. A ship running into a rock field is a ship that has nowhere else to go.


                         TORAL
          Follow them in. Maintain distance.

The Vor'nak enters the belt. Slower than Enterprise — Toral is not reckless. He threads the ship between the outer rocks with a precision that suggests this is not his first asteroid field.

Enterprise is ahead. Visible on the tactical display as a flickering contact — the mineral interference degrading the signal, the rocks cutting lines of sight.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
          Forward disruptors. Fire when you have a line.

The tactical officer fires. Two green beams lance through the belt — bright, fast, straight.

They hit rock.

An asteroid the size of a city block absorbs the first beam. The stone glows orange at the impact point, fractures, sheds debris. The second beam clips the edge of another rock and scatters — the energy dispersing into the mineral-dense dust like light through fog.

Enterprise is behind both of them. The rocks ate the shots.

Toral looks at the tactical display. Enterprise is weaving. Using the rocks as cover — ducking behind one, changing vector, appearing briefly in a gap before the next stone rolls into the line of fire.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
               (quiet — not to the tactical officer, to himself)
          They can fly.

The tactical officer's blood is up. The chase. The prey running. The weapons hot.


                         VOR'NAK TACTICAL OFFICER
          Commander — they're in the open! Between those two —

He reaches for the firing control.


                         TORAL
          Save your energy.

The tactical officer's hand stops.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
          Do not waste my disruptors on a rock field. Every shot that hits stone is a shot I don't have when it matters.

               (beat)

          We get closer.


                         VOR'NAK TACTICAL OFFICER
               (the excitement overriding the warning — the voice of a young officer who has never been hit)
          Then we close NOW! Full impulse — they're right there, we can —

Toral backhands him.

Not a shove. Not a push. The back of a closed fist, the full rotation of the shoulder behind it — a blow delivered with the specific economy of a man who has done this before and knows exactly how much force is required to end a conversation.

The tactical officer hits the bulkhead. Slides down. Sits on the deck for a moment, looking at the ceiling, reassessing his career choices.

Toral steps over him. Takes the tactical console.

His hands on the controls are not the hands of a commander. They are the hands of a weapons officer — the muscle memory of a man who started at this station and never forgot what it felt like.

He pulls up the tactical display. Studies it. The asteroid field. The corridors. Enterprise's trajectory.

The FIRST OFFICER, from the command platform:


                         VOR'NAK FIRST OFFICER
          Cloaking device, Commander. We could —


                         TORAL
               (not looking up from the display)
          An asteroid field. Without shields. You can see for yourself where that leads.

He gestures at the tactical display — the debris field where K'Tagh's warbird is still spinning, holed by rocks it couldn't deflect because it had no shields.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
          Only an idiot drops his shields in this environment when he has the advantage.

The first officer says nothing more.

And there — behind Enterprise, drifting on a slightly faster orbit than the surrounding rocks — a massive asteroid. Twelve kilometers. Iron-rich. Moving on a vector that will carry it past Enterprise's current position within four minutes.

Toral points at it. His finger on the tactical display — pressing against the glass, against the image of the rock.


                         TORAL
          No. We wait. We have the upper hand.

               (beat)

          We approach them from behind this rock.

His finger traces the trajectory — the asteroid overtaking Enterprise, the Vor'nak riding its shadow.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
          We have full shields. We can eat their shots with ease. And when we are close — we crush them. They have no chance.

He enters the course. Not toward Enterprise. Toward the asteroid.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
          Half impulse power. Heading one-seven mark two-eight-two-seven-nine.

The Vor'nak adjusts — sliding sideways, letting Enterprise pull ahead, falling behind the massive asteroid like a shark behind a whale.

On the tactical display — the geometry is simple. The big asteroid drifts forward. The Vor'nak drifts with it. Enterprise doesn't see what's behind the rock. In four minutes, the rock passes Enterprise's position and the Vor'nak is on top of them.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
               (to the bridge — not loud, not quiet, the tone of a man stating a fact)
          They are not the only ones who know how to hide.

He stands at the tactical console. Waiting. Patient. The asteroid carrying him forward like a current carrying a predator toward a shore.

Behind him, the tactical officer picks himself up off the deck. Touches his jaw. Says nothing. Returns to a secondary station.

Nobody on the Vor'nak's bridge speaks.

The asteroid drifts.


INT. ENTERPRISE-A — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


On the viewer — the Vor'nak's disruptor beams have stopped. The green flashes that were lighting up the rocks every few seconds — gone. The pursuing ship has stopped firing.

And then — it changes course. On the tactical display, the Vor'nak's contact is curving away. Not retreating. Sliding laterally, toward the denser part of the belt.

Kirk watches it.


                         KIRK
          Where are they going?


                         SPOCK
               (reading the display)
          The Vor'nak has altered course. Bearing one-seven mark two-eight. She is moving toward the inner ring.

               (beat)

          The trajectory is consistent with K'Tagh's position. It is possible Commander Toral is moving to assist his disabled vessel.

Kirk looks at the display. The Vor'nak pulling away. The distance opening.

For one moment — one breath — the thought: if they're going to K'Tagh, Enterprise could run. Back out of the belt. Into open space. Warp speed. Gone.


                         KIRK
          If they're heading for K'Tagh —


                         SPOCK
          Then their attention is elsewhere. Temporarily.

Kirk looks at the viewer. The belt. Open space behind them. The temptation of escape — a straight line to safety.


                         KIRK
          Show me K'Tagh's ship. Main viewer.


                         ENSIGN
          Sir — K'Tagh's vessel is occluded. The asteroid at bearing two-one-four is blocking our line of sight. I can't get a visual.


                         KIRK
          Damn it.

He can't see K'Tagh. He can't see the Vor'nak's destination. He can't verify Spock's hypothesis. And the window to run is closing — if the Vor'nak is not going to K'Tagh, if it's circling, if this is a feint —

He can't know.


                         KIRK (CONT'D)
          Take us deeper. Scotty — I need more shield power. Whatever you can route from non-essential systems.


                         SCOTTY (V.O.)
               (from engineering, over comms — the voice of a man who has been rerouting power for thirty years and has run out of non-essential systems to rob)
          Captain, I've already pulled everything that isn't bolted to the hull. The port generator is running on spit and prayer. I can give you maybe five percent more if I shut down the —


                         KIRK
          Do it. Whatever it is, do it.

Enterprise drifts deeper into the belt. Into the rocks. Into the corridors where the stones are continent-sized and the spaces between them are narrow and dark.

Kirk doesn't know what's behind the asteroid he can't see past. He doesn't know that Toral is riding it toward him like a current.

The belt closes around them. The rocks getting denser. The corridors narrower. The iron content in the surrounding asteroids thickening on the sensor display — the same iron that degrades communications, blinds scanners, and turns the belt into a place where you navigate by eye and die by surprise.

Hummer is watching the display. Not the Vor'nak — that contact has faded into the mineral noise. He is looking at the rocks themselves. The discoloration patterns. The iron veins.

He recognizes them.


                         HUMMER
               (quiet — to Kirk)
          Captain. If we go much deeper, we're heading into the inner ring.


                         KIRK
          I know where we are.


                         HUMMER
          The Klingon installations are in the inner ring. The bases I found. Weapons caches, energy stores — volatile material inside the rocks. If we drift too close to one of those asteroids and something hits it — or if someone fires near it —

He doesn't need to explain. Everyone on the bridge saw what happened to the asteroid in the corridor. The torpedo that turned into a bomb.


                         HUMMER (CONT'D)
          There could be more of them. I found four. There could be twelve. And I don't know which rocks they're in — I only mapped the ones I could reach in the shuttle.

Kirk looks at the viewer. The rocks ahead. Any one of them could be hollow. Any one of them could be loaded. The belt that was a hiding place is now a minefield they can't map.

Behind them — somewhere — the Vor'nak. Invisible. Approaching.

Ahead of them — rocks that might explode if anything touches them.


                         KIRK
          There are no alternatives.

               (beat)

          If they get close, we're finished. Our shields won't hold against a sustained disruptor barrage and we both know it.

He looks at Hummer. The look of a man who has calculated the odds and found them exactly as bad as they appear.


                         KIRK (CONT'D)
          Take us deeper.

Enterprise slides into the inner ring. The ancient rocks closing overhead like a canopy. The iron-rich stones with their dark veins — the ones Hummer learned to read as a miner and now reads as a man looking for bombs.

Kirk looks at the tactical display. Spock's sensor sweep from earlier — the energy anomalies he flagged hours ago, the asteroids radiating heat that their composition can't explain. They are marked on the display in amber. Warm spots in a field of cold stone.

There are seven of them. Scattered through the inner ring. And Enterprise is drifting straight toward them.


                         KIRK
          Spock. The thermal contacts on the display. The ones you flagged.


                         SPOCK
          The energy signatures remain consistent with shielded power systems. Their positions have not changed.

Kirk looks at the amber dots on the display. Seven asteroids that are warmer than they should be. Seven rocks that might be loaded with the same volatile material that turned a corridor wall into a bomb.

They are ahead. On both sides. One almost directly in Enterprise's path — a dark body fourteen kilometers across with an iron discoloration pattern Hummer would recognize if he were looking at the surface instead of the display.


                         KIRK
          Can we go around them?


                         SPOCK
          Negative. The corridor narrows at this depth. The thermal contacts bracket our trajectory on both sides. To avoid them would require reversing course.

Reversing course means going back. Back toward the Vor'nak.


                         KIRK
          Take us through. Slow. Minimum profile.

Enterprise drifts forward. Into the narrowing corridor. The warm asteroids on either side — close enough that the passive sensors register their energy output as a faint shimmer on the display. Fourteen kilometers to port. Nine kilometers to starboard. The one directly ahead — the largest — drifting slowly across their bow.

They pass it.

Close. The surface scrolling past on the viewer — dark iron veins, craters, the ordinary face of a billion-year-old rock that might be hollow, might be loaded, might be nothing.

Nobody breathes.

A boulder strikes the shields.

Not large — perhaps two meters across. A fragment shed from a larger body, tumbling through the corridor on its own small trajectory. It hits the forward shield and the shield flares — a brief blue shimmer across the viewport, the energy discharge visible for a fraction of a second before the system compensates.

The ship shudders. Barely. A tremor felt in the deck plates and nowhere else.


                         PARK
          Shields at twenty-eight percent. Forward generator compensating.

Another impact. Starboard this time. Smaller — gravel. The shield flares blue again. Dimmer this time.


                         PARK (CONT'D)
          Twenty-six.

Another. Port side. The damaged generator struggling to cover the arc. The blue flare uneven — bright on the starboard half, dark where the port emitter can't match the output.

Twenty-four.

The belt is grinding them down. Not with weapons. Not with intent. With the ordinary physics of a place where too many rocks are moving and a ship with failing shields is drifting through them. Each impact costs energy. Each flare means less power for the next one. The shields are a candle burning from both ends — the rocks from outside, the generators from inside.

Enterprise drifts through the inner ring like a wounded animal moving through thorns. Every step costs something. Every meter deeper is a meter further from safety and a meter closer to the warm rocks that might end everything.

The bridge watches the shield percentage tick down. Twenty-two. Twenty-one. Each impact accompanied by the blue flare — the visual heartbeat of a ship running out of time.


                         SCOTTY (V.O.)
               (from engineering — quieter now, the bluster gone)
          Captain. At this rate of attrition, I can give you maybe twenty minutes of shield coverage. After that we're running on hull plating and good intentions.


                         KIRK
          Understood.

He looks at the viewer. The rocks ahead. The warm asteroids on the display. The shield percentage dropping with each blue flare.

Behind them — somewhere — Toral.

The bridge is quiet. The kind of quiet that comes from knowing you are between two things that can kill you, moving toward one because the other is worse.


EXT. ASTEROID BELT — INNER RING — CONTINUOUS


The massive asteroid — Toral's cover — drifts forward through the field. Twelve kilometers of stone carrying a warship in its shadow.

Then the asteroid's horizon breaks.

The Vor'nak rises from behind it.

Not fast. Not slow. The deliberate vertical ascent of something that has been waiting and is now done waiting. The bow clears the rock face first — the forward disruptor bank, the armored deflector, the angular Klingon hull emerging over the asteroid's edge like a predator rising over a hill.

Klingon music. The war drums. The brass. The sound that vibrates in the sternum.

The Vor'nak is close. Much closer than before. The asteroid carried it forward while Enterprise drifted deeper — the distance has halved. Enterprise fills the Vor'nak's tactical display now, not as a postage stamp but as a target. A real target with readable hull markings and visible damage.


INT. IKS VOR'NAK — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


                         VOR'NAK TACTICAL OFFICER
               (the new one — the first is still touching his jaw)
          Enterprise! Dead ahead! Range four thousand meters!

Toral is already at the scope. A targeting periscope — the particular Klingon affectation of a manual optical system built into the tactical station, used by commanders who trust their eyes more than their sensors.

He looks through it. Enterprise in the crosshairs. The damaged hull. The dark navigation array. The port nacelle with its uneven glow. A ship that has been running for too long and has nowhere left to run.

He pulls back from the scope.


                         TORAL
               (quiet — almost gentle)
          DaHjaj 'Hegh motlh QaQ jaj.

The Klingon proverb. Today is a good day to die. Said not about himself — about them.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
          Goodbye, Kirk.

He fires.

Three disruptor beams. Green. Blinding. The full output of the Vor'nak's forward bank at four thousand meters.

The first beam misses. It passes over Enterprise's saucer — close enough to light the hull in green — and strikes an asteroid ahead and to port. The rock detonates. Not the catastrophic explosion of a hidden base — just stone. But enough. The asteroid shatters into dust, the debris cloud expanding forward of Enterprise's position.

The second beam misses wide. Starboard. It hits another rock — this one smaller, closer to Enterprise's trajectory. The impact blasts a crater in the surface and sends a spray of fragments tumbling across Enterprise's bow.

The third beam hits.

Aft section. The engineering hull between the nacelle pylons. The shields flare — not blue. White. The last reserve of the failing generators discharging in a single burst that lights up the entire ship for one frame.

The shields die.

The white flare gutters and goes out. The shield perimeter — visible for a fraction of a second as a complete sphere of energy around the hull — collapses inward and vanishes.

Enterprise is naked.


INT. ENTERPRISE-A — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


Every console on the bridge flashes red simultaneously.


                         PARK
          Shields down! Shields are down!

The dust cloud from the shattered asteroids ahead — the ones Toral's missed shots destroyed — rolls over Enterprise. Fragments hit the hull. Not the shields. The hull. Metal on metal. The sound is different — not the muffled thump of an energy barrier absorbing impact. A hard, sharp crack. The sound of things hitting the skin of the ship.

Rocks raining on the hull like hail on a tin roof. Each one a dent, a scratch, a divot in the hull plating that was never meant to be the last line of defense.


                         KIRK
          Suggestions?

One word. Said to the bridge. Said to anyone.

Hummer looks at the tactical display. One second. The second stretches.

The Vor'nak behind them. Recharging for the next volley. Fifteen seconds, maybe twenty, before Toral fires again. And this time there are no shields.

The warm asteroids on the display. The amber dots. The Klingon installations inside the iron-rich rocks. Scattered through the inner ring. Three of them — three — now behind Enterprise. Between Enterprise and the Vor'nak.

Between Enterprise and the Vor'nak.

Hummer looks up. His eyes are different. The expression of a man who has just seen the board from above instead of from behind his own pieces.


                         HUMMER
               (a whisper — the voice of a man quoting himself from a dinner table a lifetime ago)
          Make the battle meaningless.


                         KIRK
          What?


                         HUMMER
          Their bases. The ones in the rocks. If they're loaded the way the one in the corridor was — they're bombs. Massive bombs. And three of them are between us and the Vor'nak right now.

He pulls up the tactical display. The amber dots. Three of them strung across the corridor behind Enterprise like mines in a channel.


                         HUMMER (CONT'D)
          We fire torpedoes into those rocks. All of them. Every tube we have. The explosions won't just destroy the asteroids — they'll create a blast wave that nothing in this corridor survives. Not us if we're too close. Not them if they're where they are.


                         KIRK
          That's not a plan. That's a demolition.


                         HUMMER
          Yes sir. It is.

               (beat)

          What else have we got?

Kirk looks at him. Looks at the display. The Vor'nak recharging. Twelve seconds now.

Three warm asteroids behind them. Klingon installations. Volatile material. Bombs.


                         KIRK
          Do it. Quick.


                         HUMMER
          Aft torpedo tubes — how many?


                         PARK
          Two. Loaded.


                         HUMMER
          Load all remaining. Continuous fire, aft tubes. Three targets — I'm uploading coordinates now.

His hands on the console. The targeting solutions — not calculated. Remembered. He mapped those asteroids from a mining shuttle six hours ago. He knows their positions the way he knows the surface of every rock he ever cut.

Three coordinates. Three warm asteroids.


                         HUMMER (CONT'D)
          Fire.

The first torpedo launches aft. A bright point racing away from Enterprise, shrinking into the dark corridor behind them.

The second. Three seconds later. Different vector — angling toward the second asteroid.

The third. The last tube cycles and fires. The torpedo streaks toward the third amber dot on the display.

Enterprise shudders with each launch. The recoil of a ship that has nothing left to give except the weapons it's throwing away.


INT. IKS VOR'NAK — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


Toral sees the torpedoes on his display. Three contacts. Launched aft. Heading toward him.

He watches them come. The trajectories are clear on the screen — three bright lines converging on his position from the direction of Enterprise.

He almost smiles.


                         TORAL
          They're desperate.

The first torpedo reaches the Vor'nak's position. It streaks past the main viewport — close enough that the bridge lights up orange for a frame, close enough that the tactical officer flinches — and continues past. It doesn't detonate. It isn't aimed at the Vor'nak.

It hits the asteroid behind them.


                         TORAL (CONT'D)
               (watching the torpedo pass)
          You've lost your touch, Kirk.


INT. ENTERPRISE-A — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


On the viewer — the aft view. The torpedoes racing away. The Vor'nak visible now, a dark shape in the corridor, close enough to see the green glow of its weapons cycling for the second volley.

The first torpedo passes the Vor'nak. Misses. Continues into the darkness.

Kirk watches. Hummer watches. The bridge watches.

The torpedo hits the asteroid.


EXT. ASTEROID BELT — CONTINUOUS


The asteroid does not shatter.

It detonates.

The hidden Klingon installation — weapons caches, energy cells, three years of volatile material stored in chambers carved into the iron core — amplifies the photon torpedo the way a bullet amplifies a primer. The rock splits open from within. The iron core blazes white. A sphere of energy expands outward from the center at a speed that turns the surrounding dust into plasma.

The second torpedo hits its asteroid. Two hundred meters to the right. The same result — but this installation is larger. The explosion overlaps with the first. Two spheres of white energy merging into a wall.

The third torpedo hits.

The third asteroid is the largest of the three. The deepest installation. The one Hummer's shuttle sensors registered as the strongest thermal signature. When it detonates, the blast dwarfs the other two combined.

Three explosions becoming one. A wall of energy and rock and burning ordnance expanding through the corridor like a river breaking a dam. Silent — in space, all of this is silent — but the scale of it communicates its own sound. The visual weight of a million tons of rock and fire moving outward at a velocity that nothing in the corridor can outrun.

The shockwave hits the Vor'nak broadside.

The cruiser — full shields, full power, every system at maximum — takes the wave like a ship takes a rogue sea. The shields hold for one frame. Two frames. The green shimmer blazes so bright the ship disappears behind its own defenses.

Then the shields collapse under the sustained force of three detonations and a corridor's worth of rock traveling at a significant fraction of the speed of sound in vacuum — which is to say, carried by the expanding plasma, not by air, and therefore arriving not as a gust but as a wall.

The Vor'nak flips. Not gradually. The blast wave catches the port side and rotates the entire ship — nose over tail, the hull spinning through ninety degrees in two seconds. The engines — still firing, still at half impulse — are now pointing sideways, driving the ship into a flat spin that the inertial dampeners cannot compensate for.

Rocks follow the shockwave. The larger fragments — boulders, hull-sized chunks of iron-rich stone, pieces of the installations themselves — traveling behind the energy front like shrapnel behind a grenade. They arrive three seconds after the wave and they arrive into a ship that is already spinning, already exposed, already shield-less.

They begin to tear it apart.


INT. ENTERPRISE-A — BRIDGE — CONTINUOUS


Enterprise takes the shockwave from the front. It hits the saucer first — the unshielded hull absorbing the force with nothing between the energy and the metal except the metal itself.

The ship lurches. Hard. Kirk is thrown from the chair. Officers grab consoles, railings, anything bolted down. The overhead lighting fails entirely — the bridge plunged into darkness for two full seconds before the emergency systems engage and the red battle lighting comes up.

A boulder — ten meters across, a fragment from one of the detonated asteroids — strikes the port warp nacelle. The impact is visible on the bridge — through the viewport, a flash of orange and a shower of debris spraying outward from the nacelle pylon. The ship shudders sideways.

Another rock. Smaller. It hits the saucer section aft of the bridge module — the impact transmitting through the hull as a deep metallic groan, the sound of a ship's skeleton absorbing something it was not designed to absorb.

More debris. A rain of fragments striking the hull — the same hail that ground down the shields, now hitting bare metal. Each impact a bright spark visible through the viewport. The sound — constant now, a drumroll of stone on steel.

Enterprise spins. Not as violently as the Vor'nak — the shockwave was weaker at this distance, attenuated by the corridor walls and the rocks between them. But enough to push the ship thirty degrees off axis, the stars wheeling in the viewport, the inertial dampeners screaming in frequencies the crew can feel in their joints.

Then — it passes.

The shockwave dissipates. The debris thins. The drumroll fades to scattered impacts. Enterprise stabilizes — not smoothly, not cleanly, the attitude control fighting to bring the ship level against the residual forces.

Kirk pulls himself back into the chair. Blood on his hand — he cut it on something. He doesn't notice.


                         KIRK
          Report.


                         PARK
          Hull breaches on decks seven and twelve. Port nacelle is — I'm reading structural damage to the pylon. Warp drive is offline. Impulse — starboard is functional. Port is gone.

               (beat)

          We're alive, sir.


                         KIRK
          The Vor'nak.


                         HUMMER
          Viewer.

The aft viewer engages. The corridor behind them — transformed. Where there were asteroids there is now a churning cloud of dust and debris and the fading glow of three detonations cooling from white to orange to red. The corridor walls are scarred. Whole sections of rock have been sheared away by the blast.

And in the middle of it — the Vor'nak.

Spinning. Slowly. The flat spin induced by the shockwave gradually decaying into an irregular tumble as the debris impacts knock the rotation off axis. The hull is intact — Klingon engineering, built to survive — but the systems are not. No shield glow. No engine glow. The running lights flickering in patterns that suggest cascading power failures.

She is not destroyed. She is stopped. A ship without propulsion, without shields, without weapons power, tumbling slowly in a debris field that will grind her down the way it ground down Enterprise's shields — one impact at a time, one dent at a time, until the hull can't take any more.

Kirk looks at the viewer.


EXT. SPACE — CORDIA ASTEROID BELT — CONTINUOUS


Enterprise. Close. Filling the frame.

The ship is damaged. The port warp nacelle is dark — the pylon bent at an angle that no engineer designed, a boulder-sized dent in the housing where the rock hit. The saucer section bears the scars of a hundred impacts — scratches, dents, a cracked viewport on deck four. Atmosphere is venting from two breach points in thin white streams.

She is battered. She is broken in places.

She is alive.

The camera pulls back. Slowly. Enterprise recedes — still large, still present, still the center of the frame. The damage visible but not dominant. A ship that took everything the belt could throw at it and is still here.

Further back. The debris field becomes visible. The churning cloud. The orange glow.

And there — in the cloud — the Vor'nak. Small now. A dark shape tumbling in the dust. The size of a model. The size of a toy.

Further still. The corridor widens. And in the far distance — barely visible, a point of light catching the sun on each slow rotation — K'Tagh's warbird. Still spinning where Enterprise left it. Still holed. Still dark.

Two Klingon warships. One tumbling in a dust cloud. One spinning with boulders in its hull. Both the size of matchbox cars against the scale of the belt. Small. Helpless. The particular insignificance of things that were dangerous an hour ago and are now just objects drifting in the ordinary physics of a place that doesn't care about any of them.

Enterprise in the foreground. Damaged but whole. Larger than anything else in the frame.

The belt continues its ancient rotation around them all.
